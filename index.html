Self-learning knowledge bases

When AI chat fails and a human agent resolves a ticket, nobody goes back to check what went wrong with the KB. The article stays broken until someone randomly notices. This model proposes a system that connects those dots — and for customers without a KB, creates articles from what agents already tell users.

The model

Every human-resolved ticket is a signal that the KB failed. The system classifies why it failed, then acts accordingly. Six classifications, two resolution types, two customer journeys — unified in one pipeline.

Two resolution types, two paths

Informational resolution

Agent provides knowledge: explains a policy, corrects a fact, gives a how-to. This feeds KB articles — corrections to existing ones or seeds for new ones.

Action-based resolution

Agent performs an action: resets credentials, provisions a tool, grants access. The useful article isn't "how to reset" — it's "this requires a human agent, here's what to expect."

Two customer journeys

Customer A: has existing KB

Problem is maintenance. Articles go stale. "Your HSA article is wrong — here's the fix, based on 3 agents who corrected it."

Customer B: building from scratch

Problem is they have no articles. "Your agents answered 8 GitHub 2FA questions this month. Here's a draft article."

Same pipeline, different output. Correction path: article + transcript → find what's wrong → draft the fix. Creation path: transcript (no article) → extract knowledge → draft new article. The infrastructure is shared.

Two intervention points

Immediate: on that ticket

Right after resolution: "You referenced info not in any KB — want to save this as an article?" Catches the agent while context is fresh.

Retroactive: on the KB page

Analysis across resolved tickets. "This article was surfaced 50 times, escalated 38. Here are the conversations." Patterns become visible.

Known limitations and open questions

What the system cannot see

Echo problem

Agent trusts wrong article, repeats wrong answer. Zero delta. System thinks everything is fine. Especially dangerous for health benefits, compliance, financial data.

Mitigation: agent feedback channel, time-based decay, external source monitoring.

Silent deflection

AI gives wrong answer, user accepts. No ticket created. Deflection rate looks healthy while answer is wrong.

Mitigation: AI conversation logging + retroactive analysis when articles are corrected.

Incident vs KB signal

Multiple tickets about the same topic could mean a bad KB article OR a system outage. Five tickets about HSA limits over three weeks = KB pattern. Five tickets about Slack access in two hours = incident. The system needs to distinguish informational queries from failure reports — velocity, resolution type, and topic language are the candidate differentiators.

What the admin should see on the article page: "This analysis covers tickets where a human agent intervened. If AI resolved a query using this article and the user accepted, the system cannot verify whether that answer was correct."

Questions for engineering alignment

Are we logging which KB article gets surfaced per ticket? This is the prerequisite for everything.
Does the model make sense? The six classifications, two resolution types, two customer journeys, two intervention points, the blind spots. What's missing? What's wrong?
What's EA scope? Foundation (visibility only, no LLM)? Foundation + classification + diagnosis? Does draft generation make it into EA?
How does the system classify resolution type? LLM auto-classify (accurate, costs per ticket) vs structured agent input (adds friction) vs hybrid?
Does the creation path ship alongside correction in EA? Different LLM prompt, same infrastructure. Potentially more valuable for customers without a KB.

How we got here

A. Initial exploration: L0–L7 spectrum

We started by mapping every possible approach from fully manual to fully autonomous. Eight levels, each representing a different degree of AI involvement in KB maintenance.

LevelApproachWhat it does
L0KB stress testAdmin manually selects tickets, runs them against KB. AI diagnoses why articles failed.
L1Agent feedbackAgents rate KB articles after resolution. Threshold alerts.
L2Staleness detectionSystem flags old/low-deflection articles. Simple heuristics.
L3Resolution draftsLLM compares resolved tickets to KB articles, drafts updates.
L4Copy-paste diffsDetect when agents modify KB content before sending.
L5Source syncMonitor upstream docs for changes.
L6Gap detectionCluster unmatched tickets, propose new articles.
L7Auto-publishHigh confidence changes publish without review.

B. The reframe: levels → layers

After jamming with Derek, we realized the eight levels weren't separate features — most were inputs to one real feature. We stopped thinking in levels and reframed as layers of the same system. Each layer builds on the last. Each is independently shippable. The "L3 Resolution Drafts" became the core, with agent feedback, staleness detection, and gap detection becoming signals that feed into it rather than standalone concepts.

Derek's feedback from our first session: "L3 is doable from our POV" and "maps closely to current thinking." Key concerns: where to surface recommendations, notification workflows, KB-queue relationship evolution.

C. Converging with engineering

Derek independently developed a model with three classifications (Staleness, Gap, Logic/Action Limitation), a four-phase pipeline (Ingestion → Verification → Synthesis → Assignment), and a strategy for distinguishing search failures from content gaps. His model's key contribution: the "Action Limitation" category (resolutions requiring human authority aren't KB problems) and the Verification step (re-scan KB before concluding an article is missing).

The unified model merges both: six classifications (Derek's three expanded with Kritika's "incomplete" and "too complex" distinctions), two resolution types (Kritika's informational vs action-based split), two customer journeys, two intervention points, and the blind spots neither model initially covered.
